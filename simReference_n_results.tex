\chapter{Platform Characterisation}
\label{chap:simulation_results}

This chapter characterizes the principal platform parameters that determine the retrieval performance of the simulated quantum memories. Their influence is analysed independently to identify the dominant transport and dephasing mechanisms and establish the relevant design trade-offs

\section{Transport-Induced Decoherence}
\label{sec:transport_dynamics}
\newcommand{\nitrogen}{\ensuremath{\mathrm{N_2}} }

Atomic transport changes the spatial distribution of the stored collective excitation and therefore its overlap with the optical mode selected during retrieval. The transport mechanisms have already been introduced as part of the model; the purpose of this section is to determine how the relevant experimental parameters affect the normalized fibre-coupled retrieval. In the warm-vapour system, the motion of Cs atoms is controlled primarily through the buffer-gas pressure, whereas in the cold-atom system it is determined by the ensemble temperature. These parameters also have different experimental roles. The buffer-gas pressure is fixed during cell fabrication, while the temperature of a cold ensemble can be adjusted through the cooling and trapping sequence.

\subsection{Buffer-Gas Pressure}
\label{sec:results_buffer_gas_pressure}

A large optical depth strengthens the collective light--matter interaction, but it also complicates the preparation of a highly spin-polarised ensemble. In a warm vapour, the optical depth is commonly increased by raising the cell temperature, which increases the Cs number density. During optical pumping, atoms undergo repeated cycles of excitation and spontaneous decay. Some of the emitted photons remain resonant with transitions from the ground-state manifold and can be reabsorbed by other atoms before escaping the cell. Their repeated absorption and re-emission redistribute the atomic population and oppose accumulation in the desired ground state. This process, known as radiation trapping, becomes increasingly important as the optical depth, and therefore the vapour temperature, is increased \cite{HeRmbsrt_ThomasEtAl_2017}.

A buffer gas addresses both this optical-pumping limitation and the transport of Cs atoms. Velocity-changing collisions reduce the diffusion coefficient and slow the escape of atoms from the optical interaction region. Molecular \nitrogen can additionally suppress radiation trapping through collisional quenching, which reduces the production of resonant fluorescent photons. The buffer-gas species and pressure must therefore be selected by considering both the required diffusion time and the optical-pumping conditions of the vapour cell.

The buffer-gas collisions must preserve the ground-state spin coherence sufficiently well for this approach to remain useful. Spin-relaxation measurements show that collisions of ground-state alkali atoms with noble gases and molecular \nitrogen produce comparatively low rates of population transfer between the ground states, although the associated spin-relaxation rate is not strictly zero \cite{HeRmbsrt_ThomasEtAl_2017}. Molecular \nitrogen offers an additional advantage over noble-gas buffers because it provides a channel for collisional quenching. During a collision, an excited Cs atom can transfer its energy to the vibrational degrees of freedom of the \nitrogen molecule and return to the ground state without emitting a resonant photon. The resulting reduction in resonant fluorescence suppresses radiation trapping and allows high spin polarisation to be maintained at temperatures for which the vapour would otherwise become difficult to pump efficiently \cite{HeRmbsrt_ThomasEtAl_2017}.

The pressure dependence considered in the simulation arises from the diffusion coefficient introduced in Eq.~\eqref{eq:diffusion_coefficient}. At fixed temperature, the adopted scaling gives $D\propto 1/ {P}$. Thus, increasing the \nitrogen pressure therefore reduces the distance over which Cs atoms diffuse during a given storage interval. From Eq.~\eqref{eq:diffusion_msd}, the characteristic diffusion length is $ \ell_{\mathrm{D}}(t)=\sqrt{6Dt}$ so that the time required to diffuse across a characteristic transverse mode size \(w_{\mathrm{eff}}\) scales approximately as
%
\[
    \tau_{\mathrm{D}}
    \sim
    \frac{w_{\mathrm{eff}}^{2}}{6D}
    \propto P.
\]
%
A higher pressure consequently delays the redistribution of the stored excitation away from the region in which the signal, control, and collected retrieval modes overlap. The atomic contributions remain weighted towards the initially prepared optical mode for longer, and the fibre-coupled retrieval decreases on a longer timescale.

The pressure scan in Fig.~\ref{fig:pressure_scan_coupling_decay} follows the dependence expected from the diffusion model. At low pressure, the larger diffusion coefficient allows Cs atoms to leave the high-overlap region rapidly, producing a comparatively fast reduction of \( \eta\). Increasing the pressure reduces the diffusion length reached at each storage time and shifts the decay towards longer times. The curves therefore show that, when only motional transport is considered, a larger buffer-gas pressure improves the persistence of the fibre-coupled retrieval mode.

This monotonic improvement does not imply that the highest available \nitrogen pressure is the best experimental choice. Collisions with the buffer gas also broaden and shift the optical transitions. Thomas \emph{et al.} report a pressure-broadening coefficient for Cs in \nitrogen that is approximately twice that obtained with Ne, illustrating the trade-off between diffusion suppression and optical broadening \cite{HeRmbsrt_ThomasEtAl_2017}. As the optical coherence linewidth increases, the resonant absorption profile and the EIT response are modified. Maintaining a comparable transparency, efficiency, and acceptance bandwidth generally requires a larger control-field Rabi frequency and therefore greater control power \cite{DeRoseEtAl_2023,EsguerraEtAl_2023}.

Pressure broadening also increases the spectral range over which the strong control field can excite the vapour. This can enhance fluorescence and other control-induced background processes and places more demanding requirements on the spectral and polarisation filtering used to separate the retrieved signal from the control field \cite{EsguerraEtAl_2023}. These restrictions are particularly relevant when the memory operates close to the single-photon level, where even a weak increase in optical background can reduce the signal-to-noise ratio.

Figure~\ref{fig:pressure_scan_coupling_decay} thus quantifies the transport-side benefit of increasing the buffer-gas pressure under otherwise fixed optical conditions. The experimental pressure must balance this improvement against the accompanying pressure broadening, control-power requirement, and optical background. Because the pressure is fixed when the cell is fabricated, a practical choice is the lowest pressure that provides the required transport-limited retrieval time while remaining compatible with the available control power, optical bandwidth, and noise requirements.

\subsection{Cold-Atom Temperature}
\label{sec:results_cold_atom_temperature}

The role played by buffer-gas collisions in the warm vapour is replaced by the thermal velocity distribution in the cold ensemble. After the cooling and trapping stage, the atoms are assumed to evolve freely during storage according to the ballistic model introduced in Eq.~\eqref{eq:ballistic_position_update}. The ensemble temperature therefore determines how rapidly the stored excitation expands away from the optical mode and loses its spatial and phase overlap with the retrieved field.

For a thermal ensemble, each Cartesian component of the atomic velocity has a standard deviation

\begin{equation}
    \sigma_v
    =
    \sqrt{\frac{k_{\mathrm{B}}T}{m}},
    \label{eq:cold_atom_thermal_velocity}
\end{equation}

where \(m\) is the atomic mass. The characteristic ballistic displacement accumulated during a storage time \(t\) is consequently $\ell_{\mathrm{th}}(t)=\sigma_v t. $ Where the time required for an atom to move across a characteristic transverse mode size \(w_{\mathrm{eff}}\) scales approximately as

\begin{equation}
    \tau_{\mathrm{tr}}
    \sim
    \frac{w_{\mathrm{eff}}}{\sigma_v}
    \propto
    T^{-1/2}.
    \label{eq:cold_atom_transit_time}
\end{equation}

Increasing the temperature therefore shortens the time over which the atoms remain within the region of strong signal, control, and collection-mode overlap. Thermal motion can also reduce the collective interference by displacing the atoms through the spin-wave phase pattern. The corresponding motional-dephasing timescale follows the same approximate \(T^{-1/2}\) dependence and becomes more restrictive as the spin-wave wave-vector \(\lvert\Delta\mathbf{k}\rvert\) is increased \cite{JiangEtAl_2016}. For the nearly collinear geometry considered here, \(\lvert\Delta\mathbf{k}\rvert\) is small, and the temperature dependence is expected to arise predominantly from the ballistic expansion of the stored spatial mode.

The temperature scan in Fig.~\ref{fig:cold_atom_temperature_scan} displays the expected monotonic dependence. The simulated temperature range extends from
\(\SI{1}{\micro\kelvin}\) to \(\SI{1}{\milli\kelvin}\), covering three orders of magnitude.
The scan therefore probes both deeply cooled ensembles and a deliberately warm limiting case in which ballistic transport is expected to dominate the retrieval decay. At \(\SI{1}{\micro\kelvin}\), the small thermal velocity produces only a slow redistribution of the stored excitation, and the fibre-coupled retrieval remains appreciable for the longest storage times considered. As the temperature is increased, the atoms move farther during the same storage interval and the retrieval curve decays more rapidly. At \(\SI{1}{\milli\kelvin}\), ballistic escape from the finite optical modes occurs sufficiently rapidly that the coupling time is strongly reduced.

This dependence has also been observed experimentally in laser-cooled atomic memories, where atomic motion limits the lifetime through both spin-wave dephasing and expansion of the cloud. For example, Jiang \emph{et al.} operated an ensemble at approximately \(\SI{13}{\micro\kelvin}\) and found that, after suppressing spin-wave dephasing, the remaining storage lifetime was limited by thermal expansion \cite{JiangEtAl_2016}.

The simulated range should not be interpreted as representing a Bose--Einstein condensate at every temperature. Temperatures between approximately \(\SI{1}{\micro\kelvin}\) and \(\SI{100}{\micro\kelvin}\) are representative of laser-cooled ensembles obtained using sub-Doppler cooling or optical molasses. The \(\SI{1}{\milli\kelvin}\) case provides an upper transport benchmark but would not normally describe a condensed gas. Conversely, reaching the lowest temperatures requires additional cooling stages and may reduce the available atom number, experimental repetition rate, or duty cycle.

The simulation holds the initial density distribution, optical depth, atom number, and beam geometry fixed. It therefore isolates the influence of temperature on ballistic transport rather than predicting the complete temperature dependence of the memory efficiency. In an experiment, the temperature must be selected together with the achievable atom number, optical depth, preparation time, and trapping configuration. The relevant operating point is consequently the highest temperature that still provides the required retrieval lifetime, rather than the lowest temperature achievable irrespective of the experimental cost.

\section{Boundary-Induced Decoherence}

The cell geometry introduces additional constraints that are fixed during fabrication. The transverse dimensions determine the influence of wall interactions, whereas the longitudinal dimension governs the range of motion-induced spin-wave dephasing.

\subsection{Wall Collisions and Surface Coatings}

The previous sections considered retrieval loss arising from atomic motion within the optical interaction region. This description is appropriate when the optical beams occupy only a small fraction of the cell cross-section, since atoms diffuse out of the optical mode before reaching the boundary. In a compact cell, however, the optical mode occupies a substantial fraction of the available transverse area, and atoms carrying the spin-wave coherence encounter the wall on timescales comparable to the memory lifetime. The cell boundary therefore becomes an essential part of the transport problem and must be included in the model.

During the residence time on the surface, the atom is exposed to interactions that are absent during free flight. Happer treats this type of relaxation as arising from fluctuating perturbations to the atomic Hamiltonian, which can include magnetic, electric, and spin-dependent interactions at the surface \cite{OP_Happer_1972}. For alkali atoms on uncoated glass, these perturbations are strong enough that wall collisions have historically been treated as highly depolarizing in optical-pumping experiments \cite{OP_Happer_1972}. The atom may therefore return mechanically to the vapor, but its ground-state coherence can be lost during the surface interaction.

The wall interaction must be separated into two conceptually different processes: mechanical reflection and internal-state survival. Mechanical reflection determines whether the atom remains inside the cell after reaching the boundary. Spin survival determines whether the atom still contributes coherently to the collective ground-state excitation after the collision. Happer describes relaxation in optically pumped vapors using the density matrix and treats many relaxation processes as the result of a weak fluctuating perturbation added to the atomic Hamiltonian. In the case of wall relaxation, this perturbation is not applied continuously during free flight. Instead, it acts primarily when the atom interacts with the cell surface. Thus, an atom may elastically return to the vapor while its electronic or hyperfine coherence has already been randomized.

Upon striking the wall, an atom does not necessarily rebound immediately. It may stick to the surface for a finite dwell time before desorbing back into the vapor \cite{OP_Happer_1972,Aiacoavc_ChiEtAl_2020}. During this residence time, thermal agitation can cause the atom to hop between adsorption sites. While the atom is adsorbed, local magnetic fields, electric-field gradients, spin-orbit interactions, or nuclear spins in the substrate can act as random perturbations \cite{OP_Happer_1972}. These perturbations generate random phase shifts and spin relaxation. For an uncoated glass wall, this interaction is typically strong enough that a wall encounter can be treated, to a good approximation, as a fully depolarizing event for an alkali atom. In the absence of buffer gas, the relaxation time is then closely connected to the average flight time between wall collisions and therefore scales with the characteristic linear dimension of the cell \cite{OP_Happer_1972}. This scaling is precisely why wall effects become increasingly important as the vapor cell is miniaturized.

Anti-relaxation coatings reduce this surface-induced relaxation by separating the alkali atom from the strongly perturbing glass substrate. The coating provides a molecular layer that reduces the perturbation experienced by the atom during adsorption and desorption, thereby decreasing the probability of spin randomization. If the perturbation accumulated during one surface encounter is weak, the spin state is only slightly changed after a single bounce and the atom can survive many wall bounces before losing its polarization. The coating can therefore be characterized by an average number of coherence-preserving collisions, denoted here by \(N_{\text{b}}\). Equivalently, one can describe the coating by a depolarization probability per bounce. This effective description is useful because the full microscopic surface dynamics are complex and material dependent.


Following the classification of Chi et al.~\cite{Aiacoavc_ChiEtAl_2020}, anti-relaxation coatings for alkali-vapour cells can be grouped into straight-chain alkanes, alkenes, and organochlorosilanes. Alkane coatings, including paraffin-based films, typically preserve atomic polarization over approximately $N_{\mathrm{b}}\sim10^{3}$--$10^{4}$ wall encounters, but their performance deteriorates at elevated temperatures. Alkene coatings can provide substantially larger collision-survival numbers, in some cases approaching $N_{\mathrm{b}}\sim10^{6}$, although they are also limited by thermal degradation. Organochlorosilanes, particularly octadecyltrichlorosilane (OTS), generally provide lower values of $N_{\mathrm{b}}$, but their chemically anchored structure allows operation at considerably higher temperatures.
The coating choice therefore involves a trade-off between wall-collision survival and thermal stability. Hydrocarbon coatings are advantageous when maximizing $N_{\mathrm{b}}$ is the main requirement, whereas OTS-type coatings are more appropriate when the cell must be heated strongly to obtain the required atomic density and optical depth. These values should be interpreted as characteristic experimental results rather than universal material constants, since coating performance also depends on fabrication, surface coverage, alkali exposure, cell geometry, and operating conditions. Temperature makes this trade-off central for warm-vapor quantum memories. Increasing the cell temperature raises the alkali vapor pressure and therefore the optical depth \(d\), which is beneficial for storage and retrieval efficiency. At the same time, a higher temperature increases the thermal velocity \(v_{\mathrm{th}}\), increases the diffusion coefficient and therefore the rate at which atoms sample the cell boundary, and can degrade or modify the coating itself. Temperature therefore plays two opposite roles. It improves the optical interaction by increasing atomic density, but it can reduce the spin-wave lifetime through faster motion and stronger wall-related relaxation. This creates the central optimization problem for the compact cell: the memory should operate at a temperature high enough to provide sufficient optical depth, but not so high that wall-induced relaxation or coating degradation dominates the decay.

The present model does not attempt to resolve adsorption dynamics or microscopic surface interactions. Instead, each wall encounter is represented by a single effective decoherence probability. When an atom reaches the cell boundary the proposed displacement is reflected to account for its continued motion inside the cell. Independently of this mechanical reflection, the atom undergoes a stochastic coherence test. The probability that an atom remains coherent after a wall collision is taken as

\begin{equation}
    p_{\mathrm{coh}}
    =
    1-\frac{1}{N_{\mathrm b}},
    \label{eq:coherence_probability}
\end{equation}

For each wall encounter, a uniformly distributed random number $(r)$ is generated. If $r>p_{\mathrm{coh}}$, the atom is assumed to lose its coherence and its contribution is removed permanently from the coherent spin-wave sum. Otherwise, the atom remains coherent and continues to contribute normally after the collision.

This stochastic boundary model provides a simple interpretation of the coating dependence shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:coating_bounce_scan}. All curves exhibit a similar initial decrease during the first few tens of microseconds, indicating that the early retrieval loss is dominated by atomic transport and the reduction of optical-mode overlap rather than by wall-induced decoherence. The coating dependence becomes apparent only after a significant fraction of the atoms has reached the cell boundary. For small values of $N_{\mathrm b}$, wall collisions act almost as an absorbing boundary, rapidly removing atoms from the coherent spin-wave sum and causing the retrieval efficiency to decay towards zero. As $N_{\mathrm b}$ increases, more atoms survive each wall encounter and can subsequently diffuse back into the illuminated region while retaining coherence. The retrieval decay therefore becomes progressively slower and approaches a non-zero long-time plateau. The curves converge for $N_{\mathrm b}\gtrsim10^3$, becoming nearly indistinguishable from the idealized limit of $N_{\mathrm b}\rightarrow\infty$. This indicates that, for the present compact-cell geometry, further improvements in coating performance provide little additional benefit because wall-induced depolarization is no longer the dominant loss mechanism. The remaining decay is instead governed primarily by finite-volume atomic transport and the loss of overlap between the evolving spin wave and the readout optical mode.

\subsection{Longitudinal Spin-Wave Dephasing}
\label{sec:results_cell_length_spin_wave}

The influence of the longitudinal system size is determined by its relation to the axial spin-wave wavelength,
\begin{equation}
    \lambda_{\mathrm{SW},z}
    =
    \frac{2\pi}{|\Delta k_z|},
    \label{eq:results_axial_spin_wave_wavelength}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta k_z$ is the axial component of the spin-wave wave vector. The presence of several spin-wave periods within the system does not itself reduce the retrieval. At the initial time, phase-matched readout converts the complete spatial phase pattern back into the selected optical mode, so contributions from different longitudinal positions interfere constructively. Dephasing is introduced only when atomic motion changes the phase associated with each contribution. An axial displacement $\Delta z_j(t)$ produces the phase change
\begin{equation}
    \delta\phi_{j,z}(t)
    =
    \Delta k_z\Delta z_j(t)
    =
    \frac{2\pi\Delta z_j(t)}
         {\lambda_{\mathrm{SW},z}}.
    \label{eq:results_axial_motion_phase}
\end{equation}
Different longitudinal displacements therefore generate different phases and reduce the constructive interference in the coherent sum of Eq.~\eqref{eq:array_factor_general}.

In a longitudinally confined system, the range of accessible displacement is restricted by the system boundaries. When the length $L$ is smaller than the axial spin-wave wavelength, the ensemble samples only a limited part of the spin-wave phase pattern. Complete phase averaging is therefore suppressed, and a residual coherent contribution may remain at long times. This produces a plateau in the retrieved coupling when other relaxation mechanisms are absent or weak.

As $L$ becomes comparable to or larger than $\lambda_{\mathrm{SW},z}$, a broader range of spin-wave phases can be sampled. The accumulated phases then cover a larger interval and cancel more effectively in the collective readout. The plateau is consequently reduced as the longitudinal dimension is increased. The relevant mechanism is not the number of spin-wave periods initially contained within the system, but the range of motion-induced phase changes that can be explored during the evolution.

The simulated length dependence is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:cell_length_plateau}. The calculation corresponds to a cylindrical Cs vapour cell with an internal diameter of $\SI{1}{\milli\metre}$, operated at $\SI{75}{\degreeCelsius}$ with $\SI{10}{\Torr}$ of $\mathrm{N_2}$ buffer gas. The control and signal beams are co-propagating and have FWHM diameters of $\SI{0.8}{\milli\metre}$ and $\SI{0.3}{\milli\metre}$, respectively. Although the beams propagate in the same direction, their frequencies differ by the Cs ground-state hyperfine splitting of approximately $\SI{9.19}{\giga\hertz}$. Neglecting refractive-index corrections, this gives an axial spin-wave wavelength of approximately $\lambda_{\mathrm{SW},z}
    \simeq
    \frac{c}{\nu_{\mathrm{hfs}}}
    \approx
    \SI{32.6}{\milli\metre}.
$ 
All curves exhibit a similar initial decrease, showing that the early retrieval loss is only weakly affected by the cell length and is instead dominated by transverse transport and loss of optical-mode overlap. The long-time behaviour changes systematically with $L$. The shortest cells retain a clear plateau because the allowed axial displacement spans less than one spin-wave wavelength. The plateau is strongly reduced when the cell length approaches $\lambda_{\mathrm{SW},z}$ and becomes negligible for lengths several times larger than this value. The $\SI{320}{\milli\metre}$ cell provides an effectively unconfined longitudinal range on the scale of the spin wave, allowing the motion-induced phases to cancel almost completely.
