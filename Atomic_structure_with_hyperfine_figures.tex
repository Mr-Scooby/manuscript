\section{Atomic Structure of Alkali-Metal Memories}
\label{sec:atomic_structure}


Efficient EIT storage requires two long-lived states that can support a ground-state coherence and an optically excited state that mediates the coupling to light. Alkali-metal atoms provide this structure because a single valence electron lies outside closed inner shells. Their low-energy spectra are therefore much simpler than those of many-electron atoms, although the atoms are not truly hydrogen-like. For Cs and Rb, the optical transitions used in quantum memories can often be described using a restricted set of electronic, hyperfine, and Zeeman states \cite{Oqmboeit_MaEtAl_2017}. The present work focuses specifically on Cs and Rb because they are among the most widely used atomic species in quantum-memory experiments and, importantly, both species are available within the experimental facilities of the research group. This choice therefore connects the theoretical and numerical analysis directly to the atomic systems that can be investigated experimentally.

Alkali atoms are widely used in quantum optics because their strongest optical transitions lie in the near-infrared spectral region. Narrow-linewidth diode lasers, modulators, detectors, and standard optical components are readily available at these wavelengths. These technical features are particularly important for EIT memories because the signal and control fields must address selected transitions, maintain stable frequency detunings, and allow the weak retrieved field to be separated from the strong classical control field \cite{Oqmboeit_MaEtAl_2017}.

The relevant optical transitions are the alkali D lines. In standard spectroscopic notation \cite{CsDataLineSteck,RbDataLineSteck}, the D$_1$ line corresponds to the electric-dipole transition

\begin{equation}
    n^2S_{1/2} \rightarrow n^2P_{1/2},
    \label{eq:d1_transition}
\end{equation}
while the D$_2$ line corresponds to
\begin{equation}
    n^2S_{1/2} \rightarrow n^2P_{3/2}.
    \label{eq:d2_transition}
\end{equation}
The D$_1$ line is the optical transition considered in the theoretical description used here. For \({}^{133}\mathrm{Cs}\), it is the \(6^2S_{1/2}\rightarrow6^2P_{1/2}\) transition near \(\SI{894.6}{\nano\metre}\). For \({}^{87}\mathrm{Rb}\), it is the \(5^2S_{1/2}\rightarrow5^2P_{1/2}\) transition near \(\SI{795}{\nano\metre}\)~\cite{CsDataLineSteck,RbDataLineSteck,Oqmboeit_MaEtAl_2017}.

Figure~\ref{fig:cs_rb_d1_hyperfine} makes explicit the multilevel structure from
which the effective three-level memory is selected. Both isotopes provide two
long-lived ground-state hyperfine manifolds and two excited hyperfine manifolds
on the D$_1$ line. The ground-state separations,
\(\SI{9.192631770}{\giga\hertz}\) in \({}^{133}\mathrm{Cs}\) and
\(\SI{6.834682611}{\giga\hertz}\) in \({}^{87}\mathrm{Rb}\), set the frequency
difference required between the signal and control fields. The D$_1$
excited-state splittings are considerably smaller, approximately
\(\SI{1.168}{\giga\hertz}\) for Cs and \(\SI{0.815}{\giga\hertz}\) for Rb.
Consequently, an experimental \(\Lambda\) system is formed by choosing one state
from each ground manifold and a common optically allowed excited state, while
nearby hyperfine levels can still contribute through off-resonant coupling and
optical pumping~\cite{CsDataLineSteck,RbDataLineSteck}.

The optical excited state is not suitable as the storage state. Its lifetime is short, and spontaneous emission transfers excitation into uncontrolled optical modes. Long-lived storage is instead obtained from two hyperfine levels of the electronic ground state. Hyperfine structure arises from the coupling between the nuclear angular momentum \(\hat{\mathbf I}\) and the electronic angular momentum \(\hat{\mathbf J}\). Following the standard angular-momentum description of alkali atoms~\cite{CsDataLineSteck,RbDataLineSteck}, the total atomic angular momentum is
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\mathbf F} = \hat{\mathbf I} + \hat{\mathbf J},
    \label{eq:total_angular_momentum}
\end{equation}
with eigenstates denoted by \(\ket{F,m_F}\). These states satisfy
\begin{equation}
    \hat{F}^2\ket{F,m_F}
    =
    \hbar^2 F(F+1)\ket{F,m_F},
    \label{eq:F_squared}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
    \hat{F}_z\ket{F,m_F}
    =
    \hbar m_F\ket{F,m_F},
    \label{eq:Fz_projection}
\end{equation}
where \(F\) is the total hyperfine angular momentum quantum number and \(m_F\) is its projection along the quantization axis.

For \({}^{133}\mathrm{Cs}\), the ground state \(6^2S_{1/2}\) is split into the two hyperfine manifolds \(F=3\) and \(F=4\), separated by approximately \(\SI{9.192631770}{\giga\hertz}\). At the manifold level, these two states define the natural storage pair~\cite{CsDataLineSteck}:
\begin{equation}
    \ket{g} \equiv \ket{6^2S_{1/2},F=3},
    \qquad
    \ket{s} \equiv \ket{6^2S_{1/2},F=4}.
    \label{eq:cs_memory_states}
\end{equation}
For \({}^{87}\mathrm{Rb}\), the ground state \(5^2S_{1/2}\) is split into \(F=1\) and \(F=2\), separated by approximately \(\SI{6.834682611}{\giga\hertz}\). The corresponding storage pair may be written as~\cite{RbDataLineSteck}
\begin{equation}
    \ket{g} \equiv \ket{5^2S_{1/2},F=1},
    \qquad
    \ket{s} \equiv \ket{5^2S_{1/2},F=2}.
    \label{eq:rb_memory_states}
\end{equation}
A complete experimental specification also requires the Zeeman sublevel \(m_F\), but the hyperfine manifolds already identify the two long-lived ground-state sectors that carry the memory coherence. Their lifetime is much longer than the lifetime of the optically excited state, allowing the optical excitation to be stored as a coherence between \(\ket{g}\) and \(\ket{s}\)~\cite{CsDataLineSteck,RbDataLineSteck,Apgteitiav_FinkelsteinEtAl_2023}.

The excited state \(\ket{e}\) is selected from the corresponding D$_1$ excited manifold. For Cs, \(\ket{e}\) belongs to the \(6^2P_{1/2}\) manifold, while for Rb it belongs to the \(5^2P_{1/2}\) manifold. The signal field couples \(\ket{g}\) to \(\ket{e}\), and the control field couples \(\ket{s}\) to the same excited state. The effective three-level structure is therefore
\begin{equation}
    \ket{g} \leftrightarrow \ket{e},
    \qquad
    \ket{s} \leftrightarrow \ket{e}.
    \label{eq:lambda_transitions}
\end{equation}
This shared excited state allows the two optical fields to create a Raman coherence between the two ground-state levels, which is the basis of the $\Lambda$ system used for EIT storage~\cite{Apgteitiav_FinkelsteinEtAl_2023,Oqmboeit_MaEtAl_2017}.

The allowed optical couplings are constrained by electric-dipole selection rules. For alkali D-line transitions, the electronic orbital angular momentum changes between an \(S\) and a \(P\) state. At the hyperfine level, the allowed transitions satisfy~\cite{CsDataLineSteck,RbDataLineSteck}
\begin{equation}
    \Delta F = 0,\pm1,
    \qquad
    F=0 \not\rightarrow F'=0,
    \label{eq:selection_rule_F}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
    \Delta m_F = 0,\pm1.
    \label{eq:selection_rule_mF}
\end{equation}
The value of \(\Delta m_F\) is fixed by the field polarization. A \(\pi\)-polarized field drives \(\Delta m_F=0\), while circular polarizations drive \(\Delta m_F=\pm1\). These rules determine which Zeeman sublevels participate in the optical coupling and how closely the multilevel atom approximates an isolated three-level system~\cite{CsDataLineSteck,RbDataLineSteck,Apgteitiav_FinkelsteinEtAl_2023}.

The Zeeman substructure directly affects the stored coherence. In the absence of external magnetic fields, and neglecting residual stray fields, the \(m_F\) sublevels within a given hyperfine manifold are degenerate. A homogeneous magnetic field lifts this degeneracy and defines a quantization axis. For magnetically sensitive coherences, the transition frequency between \(\ket{g}\) and \(\ket{s}\) then depends on the magnetic field through the Zeeman shift. If the field varies across the ensemble, the ground-state splitting becomes position dependent. This may be represented schematically as
\begin{equation}
    \omega_{sg} \rightarrow \omega_{sg}(\mathbf r),
    \label{eq:position_dependent_splitting}
\end{equation}
where \(\omega_{sg}\) is the angular frequency splitting between the two storage states. During storage, atoms at different positions accumulate different phases. The collective spin wave is then dephased, and the field retrieved into the phase-matched optical mode is reduced~\cite{Apgteitiav_FinkelsteinEtAl_2023,Oqmboeit_MaEtAl_2017}.

The ideal $\Lambda$ system is therefore an effective reduction of a multilevel alkali atom. Real Cs and Rb vapours contain several hyperfine and Zeeman states, and the optical fields can couple weakly to nearby off-resonant transitions. These additional couplings may produce optical pumping into unwanted states, imperfect dark-state preparation, reduced EIT contrast, or nonlinear noise. For a spin-wave retrieval model, the atomic structure enters primarily through the D$_1$ wavelength, the ground-state hyperfine splitting, the allowed optical couplings, and the existence of a long-lived \(\ket{g}\leftrightarrow\ket{s}\) coherence~\cite{Apgteitiav_FinkelsteinEtAl_2023,Oqmboeit_MaEtAl_2017}.
