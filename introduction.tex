\chapter{Introduction}

Quantum memories are light--matter interfaces that coherently map photonic quantum states onto material degrees of freedom and later retrieve them back into optical fields \cite{Qmeaara_HeshamiEtAl_2016,AWAVQMitCoSR_Rodriguez_,QmfpDp_FleischhauerEtAl_2002}. In this broad sense, a quantum memory is not simply an optical delay line, but a controlled absorption and re-emission process in which the quantum state of light is transferred to a stationary excitation without measuring or destroying the input state \cite{Qmeaara_HeshamiEtAl_2016,2__}. Photons are well suited for transmitting quantum information because they can propagate over long distances, while atoms and other matter systems provide comparatively long-lived internal states for storage and processing \cite{QmfpDp_FleischhauerEtAl_2002,Qmeaara_HeshamiEtAl_2016}. The central task is therefore to convert a flying photonic qubit into a stationary material coherence and, after a controllable delay, convert it back into a well-defined optical mode \cite{QmfpDp_FleischhauerEtAl_2002,AWAVQMitCoSR_Rodriguez_}.

Quantum memories are needed because many quantum-network protocols are probabilistic and require synchronization between distant events \cite{Qmeaara_HeshamiEtAl_2016}. In quantum repeaters, memories allow entanglement generation over shorter elementary links to be stored while the remaining links are established, thereby avoiding the exponential loss associated with direct transmission over long distances \cite{Qmeaara_HeshamiEtAl_2016,Tssqrnfgqc_GundoganEtAl_2024}. This capability is especially relevant for long-distance entanglement distribution, where direct optical transmission through fiber is limited by loss and where global-scale quantum communication may require satellite-assisted architectures \cite{Tssqrnfgqc_GundoganEtAl_2024,Smqms_JutiszEtAl_2025}. Recent proposals therefore consider quantum memories either on the ground or on-board satellites, including space-borne repeater chains and single-satellite architectures with two memories acting as a time-delayed repeater node \cite{Tssqrnfgqc_GundoganEtAl_2024}. In this context, memory times below $\SI{1}{\second}$ can already be relevant for global distances, while storage times on the order of $\SI{100}{\milli\second}$ may enable intercontinental distances above $\SI{8000}{\kilo\meter}$ in suitable satellite-based schemes \cite{Tssqrnfgqc_GundoganEtAl_2024}.

The performance of a quantum memory is described by several figures of merit, and no single platform is optimal in all of them \cite{AWAVQMitCoSR_Rodriguez_,Qmeaara_HeshamiEtAl_2016}. The most common metrics are storage-and-retrieval efficiency, fidelity, storage time, bandwidth, noise, multimode capacity, and long-term operational stability \cite{Qmeaara_HeshamiEtAl_2016,Smqms_JutiszEtAl_2025,AWAVQMitCoSR_Rodriguez_}. Efficiency measures how much of the input optical excitation is recovered, fidelity measures how accurately the quantum state is preserved, and the storage time quantifies how long the material coherence remains useful for retrieval \cite{Qmeaara_HeshamiEtAl_2016,AWAVQMitCoSR_Rodriguez_}. For practical deployment, however, these intrinsic metrics must be considered together with engineering constraints such as robustness, alignment stability, size, weight, and power consumption \cite{Smqms_JutiszEtAl_2025}. The recent demonstration of a portable, rack-mounted warm-vapour quantum memory highlights this shift from laboratory performance alone toward integrated systems that can operate outside a controlled laboratory environment \cite{Smqms_JutiszEtAl_2025}.

Quantum memories have been demonstrated in several physical platforms, including cold atomic ensembles, Bose--Einstein condensates, warm alkali vapours, rare-earth-ion-doped solids, colour centres, ions, and other solid-state systems \cite{Qmeaara_HeshamiEtAl_2016,Smqms_JutiszEtAl_2025,AWAVQMitCoSR_Rodriguez_}. Solid-state systems can offer long coherence times, high density, or compatibility with integrated devices, while cold atoms and Bose--Einstein condensates provide well-controlled motional states and narrow spectral features \cite{Qmeaara_HeshamiEtAl_2016,AWAVQMitCoSR_Rodriguez_}. Warm alkali vapours are particularly attractive for deployable quantum memories because they can provide large optical depths in relatively simple cells without laser cooling or cryogenic infrastructure \cite{Smqms_JutiszEtAl_2025}. Bose--Einstein-condensate memories and warm-vapour memories therefore represent complementary limits: the former emphasizes motional control and low temperature, whereas the latter emphasizes simplicity, scalability, and reduced experimental complexity \cite{Qmeaara_HeshamiEtAl_2016,Smqms_JutiszEtAl_2025}.

In optically controlled ensemble memories, the basic storage process can be understood using a three-level $\Lambda$ system with two long-lived ground states and one optically excited state \cite{Qmeaara_HeshamiEtAl_2016,QmfpDp_FleischhauerEtAl_2002}. A weak signal field and a strong control field couple the two optical transitions, so that the incoming photon is mapped onto a collective ground-state coherence, often called a spin wave \cite{QmfpDp_FleischhauerEtAl_2002,Qmeaara_HeshamiEtAl_2016}. The spatial phase of this spin wave is set by the wave-vector difference
%
\begin{equation}
    \Delta \mathbf{k}
    =
    \mathbf{k}_{\text{sig}}-\mathbf{k}_{\text{c}},
\end{equation}
%
where $\mathbf{k}_{\text{sig}}$ and $\mathbf{k}_{\text{c}}$ are the signal and control wave vectors. Retrieval is then a collective emission process: the stored atomic coherence is converted back into light, and efficient readout requires that the phases of the microscopic emitters add constructively into the desired optical mode \cite{QmfpDp_FleischhauerEtAl_2002,Qmeaara_HeshamiEtAl_2016}. Thus, the memory lifetime relevant for an experiment is not only the survival of internal atomic coherence, but the survival of the retrievable collective mode.

The move toward portable and space-compatible memories makes this mode-survival problem more demanding \cite{Smqms_JutiszEtAl_2025,Tssqrnfgqc_GundoganEtAl_2024}. The standalone warm-vapour system of Jutisz \textit{et al.} uses a compact integrated architecture with a $\SI{75}{\milli\meter}$-long, $\SI{4}{\milli\meter}$-diameter cesium cell containing $\SI{5}{Torr}$ of $\mathrm{N_2}$ buffer gas, together with focused signal and control beams of $\SI{120}{\micro\meter}$ and $\SI{210}{\micro\meter}$ FWHM diameter, respectively \cite{Smqms_JutiszEtAl_2025}. Such systems are promising because warm vapours have low complexity and favourable size, weight, and power requirements, but further miniaturization changes the relevant physics \cite{Smqms_JutiszEtAl_2025}. As the cell size decreases, atoms diffuse across finite optical modes more rapidly, wall collisions become more frequent, and the retrieved field becomes more sensitive to beam geometry, magnetic-field gradients, and phase matching \cite{Smqms_JutiszEtAl_2025,Qmeaara_HeshamiEtAl_2016}. In alkali-vapour cells, wall relaxation can strongly reduce spin-polarization lifetimes, and this effect becomes more pronounced in miniaturized cells because the surface-to-volume ratio increases \cite{ChiEtAl_2020}. Buffer gases and anti-relaxation coatings are therefore standard strategies for reducing wall-induced relaxation and preserving the useful atomic coherence \cite{ChiEtAl_2020,Happer_1972}. Coatings are often characterized by the average number of wall collisions an atom can undergo before depolarization; in this thesis this parameter is denoted by $N_{\text{b}}$, with typical anti-relaxation behaviour discussed for values such as $N_{\text{b}} \sim 10^3\text{ to }10^4$ and beyond \cite{ChiEtAl_2020,Happer_1972}.

This thesis investigates the decay of the retrievable spin-wave mode in realistic three-dimensional geometries, with emphasis on the power coupled back into a target fibre mode. The simulated observable is the normalized fibre-coupled retrieval power,
%
\begin{equation}
    \frac{P_{\text{fib}}(t)}{P_{\text{tot}}(0)},
\end{equation}
%
which measures the survival of the collective mode that remains phase matched and spatially overlapped with the desired optical output. This choice is essential because an ensemble can retain some total atomic coherence while losing the particular spatial phase and amplitude profile needed for efficient fibre-coupled retrieval. The model therefore treats the stored spin wave as an ensemble of moving, phased microscopic emitters whose coherent sum determines the retrieved optical field. Within this framework, diffusion, finite beam overlap, magnetic dephasing, phase mismatch, wall collisions, and coating-dependent survival are not separate effects, but different mechanisms that degrade the same retrievable collective mode. The goal is to connect microscopic atomic motion in compact warm-vapour memories to the experimentally relevant retrieval signal, providing a simulation framework for assessing how miniaturization affects quantum-memory performance in deployable and space-oriented architectures.
